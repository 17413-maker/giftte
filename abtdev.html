Why I started Gifté+

The idea started from watching family and friends struggle to pick gifts. I would see messages like "What should I get for Ma's birthday?" or "He already has everything, what now?" That stuck with me. I wanted a tool that didn't feel like a robot and didn't rely on random ads — something that actually understood people.

I sketched a simple flow on paper: ask a few smart questions, or — if the user prefers — analyze public patterns ethically to suggest gifts. The name “Gifté+” came because I wanted the product to feel friendly and a little fancy.

The early prototype was embarrassing — very basic HTML and broken CSS — but it taught me that building something is the only way to learn how to make it better.

How I learned to build it

Between school, homework, and late-night coding sessions, I learned:

  • HTML & CSS: The foundation. Layouts, responsive design, making things look good on phones and desktops.
  • Javascript & UI: For interactive elements, button states, and small animations that make the app feel alive.
  • Python & FastAPI: For backend logic, APIs, and connecting AI-powered features.
  • OSINT tools: To ethically fetch public signals (only where allowed and with consent), then safely use those signals to personalize suggestions.

I read docs, watched tutorials, and broke things many times. Every bug was a lesson — and occasionally, a victory dance.

Technical deep dive (for curious humans)

If you like how things work — here’s a friendly explanation of the stack and my approach:

Frontend

Built with simple, semantic HTML and styled with Tailwind for fast iteration. Animations are CSS-first — subtle transitions, hover states, and micro-interactions to make the app pleasant to use.

Backend

FastAPI serves endpoints for recommendations and stores minimal session data (only when users opt in). Logic is modular so that I can plug in new recommendation modules later.

Ethical OSINT

Only public, consented, and non-sensitive sources are considered. The pipeline follows strict rules: transparency, minimal retention, no scraping behind auth walls, and user consent before any analysis.

AI & Recommendation Logic

A combination of rules and AI prompts. For manual mode, the rules give shortlists and filters. For automatic mode, small models analyze signals and produce human-readable suggestions.

My school projects, my notes in margins, the late-night tutorials — it's all a part of this. I'm learning, iterating, and trying to be better every day.

A long note — things I’ve learned

Patience matters. When I started, I wanted everything to work right away. That never happens. Instead, I learned to break tasks into tiny pieces, ship small improvements, and measure progress. Celebrate the tiny wins.

Read. Not just docs, but other people's code. Seeing how someone else structured a project or solved a small problem is worth hours of trial and error.

Share early. My friends gave feedback that led to better features. If you hide your project until it's "perfect" you'll miss the chance to learn what real users want.

Be kind to yourself. Mistakes are proof you're trying. Bugs are proof you're learning. If you find a bug and fix it, you just became better than yesterday.

Build with empathy. Technical excellence is great — but building something that helps people requires understanding the humans who will use it.

These notes are not a list of rules. They're reminders I leave on my desk to help me focus when I feel stuck.
